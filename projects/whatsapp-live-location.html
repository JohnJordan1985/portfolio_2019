SUMMARY: In February 2017, as part of my BSc. in Digital Design, I created a prototype for a new interface feature, which allowed the “user to complete a specific goal.”

Pairing up with a product designer, I first proposed the “live location” feature for WhatsApp, then took ownership of the wireframe prototyping and user testing.

Later, my colleague created a high-fidelity prototype based on the results of my earlier user testing.

In October 2018, six months after we completed our project, WhatsApp released this feature with a very similiar implementation, validating our approach.

Based on the strength of our work, I was interviewed by the university about the project, which you can read about here.

When proposing new product features, I’ve always found it easier to start with problems that need solving, and work backwards to a feature that would solve them.

I’d used the static location-sharing feature of WhatsApp, but found it inconvenient that you had to stay put, or else remember to update your location if you moved. Being able to share your live location would avoid these two issues.

My colleague agreed with my suggestion, and we planned out the project. First, I would create a wireframe prototype, informed by frequent user tests. Then, my colleague would create a high-fidelity prototype, based on my specifications. Finally, I would user test the high-fidelity prototype to validate our design decisions.

After planning out the project, our first step was to generate a few different concepts for the feature, such as:

  • Requesting another user’s live location.
  • Sending your own location, without it being requested.
  • Dropping a map pin, and track people as they made their way there.
  • Dropping multiple pins on a map, and having a group of people vote on their favourite location.

Having decided on what functionality we would be prototyping, I sketched out a few interface layouts, and prepared initial task flows, which I used to structure the initial wireframe prototype.

I built the wireframe prototype using the wonderful Balsamiq, and wrote a user-testing process with an introductory script to provide context, a task list and various questions for the users.

Then, I set about user testing my designs guerilla-style: that is, informally recruiting people in public places, putting my prototype in front of them, and quickly gathering their feedback as they completed short tasks.

After each test, participants were asked a series of open-ended questions to explore their perceptions and concerns regarding the “Live Location” feature. I then iterated on my prototype, with each design decision validated with user data.

Throughout the user testing, we made a number of key design decisions:

  • Placement of the feature under the vertical ellipsis, rather than under the paper-clip like the pre-existing static location sharing. Interestingly, none of the users were aware that WhatsApp allowed them to share their static location, presumably for this reason.
  • Dropping the map pin feature, since most users made at least one error using it, causing a lot of frustration in some cases.
  • Making the feature feel “safe”, since all participants expressed various concerns regarding security when sharing their live location. We added the following:
    • a highly visible “Stop Now” button
    • clear indication that user broadcasting their location
    • automatic stop when user closes the application, and
    • an automatic time-out feature.
