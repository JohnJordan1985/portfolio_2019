SUMMARY: In July 2018, I was contacted by B. Braun, a major pharmaceutical firm, with a request for a web application.

It was to take a list of hospital patients, who were receiving total parenteral nutrition* (TPN), and return the best match amongst the company’s TPN products for each of the patients on the list.

Before my work, staff would have to match TPN products to patients manually, by reference to various charts of product ingredients: they often had hundreds of patients to match against a catalogue of over two dozen TPN products, which was obviously very time-consuming.

The project was very well received by the B. Braun team, and they praised both the visual style and ease-of-use of the application. It saved their sales staff several hours each month, manually matching patients to TPN products, making their workflow much more efficient.

When proposing new product features, I’ve always found it easier to start with problems that need solving, and work backwards to a feature that would solve them.

I’d used the static location-sharing feature of WhatsApp, but found it inconvenient that you had to stay put, or else remember to update your location if you moved. Being able to share your live location would avoid these two issues.

My colleague agreed with my suggestion, and we planned out the project. First, I would create a wireframe prototype, informed by frequent user tests. Then, my colleague would create a high-fidelity prototype, based on my specifications. Finally, I would user test the high-fidelity prototype to validate our design decisions.

After planning out the project, our first step was to generate a few different concepts for the feature, such as:

  • Requesting another user’s live location.
  • Sending your own location, without it being requested.
  • Dropping a map pin, and track people as they made their way there.
  • Dropping multiple pins on a map, and having a group of people vote on their favourite location.

Having decided on what functionality we would be prototyping, I sketched out a few interface layouts, and prepared initial task flows, which I used to structure the initial wireframe prototype.

I built the wireframe prototype using the wonderful Balsamiq, and wrote a user-testing process with an introductory script to provide context, a task list and various questions for the users.

Then, I set about user testing my designs guerilla-style: that is, informally recruiting people in public places, putting my prototype in front of them, and quickly gathering their feedback as they completed short tasks.

After each test, participants were asked a series of open-ended questions to explore their perceptions and concerns regarding the “Live Location” feature. I then iterated on my prototype, with each design decision validated with user data.

Throughout the user testing, we made a number of key design decisions:

  • Placement of the feature under the vertical ellipsis, rather than under the paper-clip like the pre-existing static location sharing. None of the users were aware that WhatsApp allowed them to share their static location, presumably for this reason.
  • Dropping the map pin feature, since most users made at least one error using it, causing a lot of frustration in some cases.
  • Making the feature feel “safe”, since all participants expressed various concerns regarding security when sharing their live location. We added the following:
    • a highly visible “Stop Now” button
    • clear indication that user broadcasting their location
    • automatic stop when user closes WhatsApp, and
    • an automatic time-out feature.

After documenting our decisions, I handed over to my colleague, who would be creating a high-fidelity prototype using Invision. In the meantime, I prepared another round of user tests to validate our earlier design decisions.

The purpose of this round of testing, was to validate the crucial design hypotheses we had formed after the initial user tests.

In a major departure from the wireframe prototype, we placed the live location feature back under the paper clip icon (where the static location feature was), rather than under the vertical ellipsis. Since this placement had resulted in poor discoverability previously, we decided to use a tooltip to advise users of this new feature.

This round of user testing was about validating our design assumptions, rather than gathering user feedback, so our metrics were quantitative in nature, e.g. 100% of users could stop the live location feature within five seconds etc.

These user tests quickly showed that:

  • There was definite user interest in the “Live Location” feature, with all thirteen participants stating they would use this feature.
  • While having the “Live Location” feature under the vertical ellipsis menu made more conceptual sense for users, they quickly discovered the feature when we implemented a tooltip, and accepted the feature’s placement under the paper clip icon.
  • Users were generally unconcerned with potential privacy issues with this feature, provided appropriate feedback and control of the feature were provided, e.g. highly visible stop button.

This was a very well-executed project, and I really enjoyed the guerilla-style usability testing that formed the core of it. It is fascinating how differently people interpret the same user interface, and this project provided plenty of divergent interpretations.

Six months after we submitted our assignment, WhatsApp launched an actual live location feature in October 2017. This gave us a chance to compare and contrast our respective implementations of the feature: there was very little difference between the two designs!

Based on the strength of our work, I was interviewed by the university about the project, which you can read about here.
